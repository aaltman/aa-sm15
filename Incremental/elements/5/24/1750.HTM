VP Engineering here. To me the first move is to shift from being hands-on to enabling other people to be hands-on. Then it’s leading leaders. At that point, to make the next leap you need to be involved in strategy. Most likely as a Sr. Manager/Director you’ll get some but not at the scale of the whole business. To get to VP/C, you are only making the decisions no one else can make.

My job is less about people and projects and more about making sure how we run the business puts the right people in the right place at the right time and that they have the tools, training, and processes to be successful. That bleeds over to sales, project management, HR…

I have an MS in engineering management (“MBA Lite”). Between that and OJT at some big companies, I have a pretty good understanding of the business as a whole so I’m confident making the decisions I need to make.

As for my day-to-day,I’m relatively new at my current company and we’re still small-ish and growing rapidly so I get involved in some strategic projects. But I always have the objective of removing myself by getting the team up to speed and the client happy with the team.

That’s probably something that has been pretty consistent in my career: my objective in every job, starting from my first internship, was to eliminate the need for that job to be done. That’s still my objective. When I walk away from this job, it will be much less work for the next person. If I don’t retire, I’ll go to another company that needs change. I have no interest in working for a company that is already huge and just trying to get even bigger.

Edit: Someone DMed me to get more details so wanted to share some of that too…

Culture is something you can’t control. You can influence but can’t control. I still do a one hour intro with all the new engineers and I tell them that: all I can do is talk - they have to take action if they see something that goes against our culture. If they don’t, we aren’t the kind of company we say we are.

For me, this job is about 1-2 things I really believe in and will die on my sword for. The rest is no different from all the other jobs I’ve had: make the best decision I can based on the information and experience I have. I’ve been lucky to have had access to execs at really big companies since my first job. I’ve been influencing them and building trust with them since I started working in tech. Now that they’re my peers (I consider the CEO, my boss, to be my peer too) so not much has changed: I share my opinions and recommendations and back them up.

..the most important part of the job is getting the right people in the right place. I’m my case…, that means defining how the structure will handle growth - who will do what now but then how will it scale. The rest will happen from there.

Second most important is how you do the work. For me, that means getting agile “right” - getting beyond ceremonies and actually delivering WITH the clients iteratively but while still managing cost, schedule, and risk of the project. That sounds like “not my job” for Engineering, but that’s our only job. If we can’t do that, it doesn’t matter how great our code is.
