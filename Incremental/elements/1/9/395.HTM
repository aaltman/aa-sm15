: How does the mental computation work?

In problem solving, it is helpful to understand the concept of conceptual computation. There are many models of computation, e.g. a Turing Machine. Here I present a simple model that will help me explain how the brain works in problem solving. It will also help me explain the problem solving strategies presented in this text.

The brain solves problems using its concept network, which is a network of ideas in mind.

A concept is an object or idea that lights up in your mind. A concept is represented by a set of brain cells that are activated upon the recognition of, or whilst thinking about the object or idea. When you think about a forest, a forest concept in your brain gets activated. At the very lowest neural level, a concept may be represented by a single neuron.

Concepts can be associated by means of concept links. In the brain, a link is a simple connection between concepts or cells. The concept of a forest is linked with the concept of a tree because forests are made of trees. The concept of a tree is linked with the concept of a branch. When you think of a tree, you increase the probability of thinking about the forest because the two form a concept link.

A concept map is a set of concepts connected by links that form more complex ideas in reasoning. When you think about having a nice time in a forest, your brain lights up a concept map that will include concepts like: forest, self (i.e. you), pleasure, and perhaps other associated concepts like: blue sky, warmth, butterflies, flowery smells, bird songs, etc. A good time in a forest may feel complex and complete, full of details and experiences. However, in reality, it is just an amazingly small number of concept maps lighting up in the brain. Our minds convert map simplicity into a perceived complexity of experience.

The brain is a parallel machine, i.e. it can do many things at the same time, and can activate many concept maps at the same time. However, in the thought process, parallel activations are focused into a single window of attention. Whilst thinking, you follow a specific reasoning pathway that rarely branches out to start two or more parallel active computations. In general, it is not possible to seed your brain with a couple of problems to solve and work on them consciously in parallel. It is possible to switch, to multitask, but this is not true parallel computing. A single line of reasoning helps prevent the interference between concept map activations. The same concept in a different context may activate a different set of concepts. Intermingling activations are beneficial primarily at times when we need a creative disruption (see later).

A second reason why we need focused attention on problems at hand is that the brain evolved as a central command organ. Its purpose is not to solve dozens of independent problems. Its main purpose is to decide the next step of action. For that sake, it collects available data from the senses, integrates the data, and executes a plan. For the brain, the main goal is to find the next step in the currently executed plan. Parallel processes will be autonomous and/or subservient. Parallel processing may convert visual signals to a concept map representing an apple, its location, and physical availability. This translates to a concept "food within reach", which helps execute the next step for a hungry animal, e.g. reaching for food. The same animal might have had some reproductive plan on its mind, but the brain will always go for a specific executive action. The reproductive plan and the corresponding concept map may remain in the state of high activation, but it won't be part of conscious processing, unless on some multitasking principle. In all likelihood, the well activated map will make the animal return to the reproductive plan once the apple has been reached.

An active map is any concept map in the state of higher activation. It is sending signals to other portions of the brain. There may be dozens or hundreds of maps active at the same time. However, very few of those maps will affect conscious experience, and only one map will play a truly central role: the focus map.

The focus map is an active concept map that has a direct access to the chief executive. That map represents what you are currently thinking about.

A brain state is a set of active concept maps with one map in focus.

A brain state transition is a change of the focus map, or the activation of new maps based on spreading of activation with gradual fading of all activations left behind.

Concept association is a new link between active concept maps. It corresponds with a new idea. An association forms a new concept link that may be used in the future. New links will affect the computation. All links are subject to valuation. Future brain transitions are more likely to follow links that have been stabilized by high valence. New associations leave a trail of progression towards the solution. Obviously, new associations can also lead to a blind alley.

A breakthrough association is a new link established between concept maps. This link is an equivalent of a piece of knowledge that determines the solution.

In the brain, conceptual computation (i.e. thinking) is a sequence of brain state transitions with one changing concept map in the focus of attention at each state. Can we choose and decide how to run the computation? Hardly! Our conscious planning of the computation is just an illusion. In reality, the computation is the expression of maps we wake up with, our general goals that are represented by maps that easily light up, and sub-goals that might be even easier to activate. On waking, a great morning idea may pop up first in the brain, then, unpredictably, most excitable active maps will light up. This first morning map may have a form of "my usual morning routine", "my usual basic goal(s)", etc. Then there are things that come up because of news on TV, or messages on the phone, or aches and pains in the body, or worries, which have a form of easily activated maps that can invade your thoughts at any minute.

The thinking process is nothing other than a computation. We move between different brain states.

Let's call the concept map associated with the problem to solve a problem map. The first thing we can do for problem solving is to keep the problem map active. If this is the first map that wakes up with you, you are already on a good path! If you need a reminder, you will not do that well. All your daily activities, all knowledge, all inspirations, must be in close connection with your map. The problem map needs to be like a predator ready to snatch any inspiration and mold itself upon the influence. If you see Trump in the news, you see him as a component of your problem solving. If this is a health problem, you might wonder if Trump is suffering too. If this is a technical problem, you might wander what Trump's arsenal or people around him could do if they faced a similar issue.

The more processing time you provide, and the more inspiration contexts you feed to your problem map, the greater the chance a given day will be notched as an incremental step towards the solution. When you go to sleep, your brain is actually going to work. Your brain will consume what you produce during the day. It is helpful to go to sleep in peace, and without the influence of medication, alcohol, and the like.

Conceptual computation is a simple chain of connected concepts streaming through your mind. Even the most complex problems can be reduced to simple links between simple ideas
